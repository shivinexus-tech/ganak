# Ganak religious-content policy

**Owner direction — 2026-07-20. Applies to every agent and every Hindu observance.**

## Product position

Ganak describes Hindu religious practice faithfully. It does not weaken, replace,
or judge a traditional observance because the practice is strict. Ganak respects
the user's belief, family tradition, sampradaya, region, and chosen level of
observance.

## Devotional guide voice — owner clarification 2026-07-22

Every festival and vrat page must feel like a trusted, encouraging worship guide,
not an essay about the product's research process. The reader should leave knowing
what the observance means, how to prepare, how to worship, what to offer or eat,
which story to read or hear, and how to complete it.

- Lead positively with the observance's sacred meaning and the next useful action.
- Use concise, direct guidance: “A simple or vegetarian meal until the recitation
  ends is generally advised,” not “many families…” followed by a rebuttal.
- Give a complete, practical household path for a person who has no elder or
  advertisement-free source to consult. Name materials, preparation, ordered
  worship steps, suitable prayer/text/story, naivedya, aarti, paran and completion
  where sources establish them.
- Honour family, regional, temple and sampradaya choices warmly. Mention a variant
  when it helps the user choose or perform the observance; do not use variants as a
  reason to avoid giving useful guidance.
- Keep each observance's unique identity, mood, deity, story, timing and practice.
  Shared schema and reusable components must not produce interchangeable prose.
- State uncertainty internally in source notes and tests. Do not expose product
  self-defence such as “not interchangeable,” “do not invent,” “not a substitute
  for authority,” “do not assume,” or explanations of why Ganak omitted a rule.
- Do not address the user like a regulator, event organiser or researcher. Generic
  instructions about access, queues, police, committees, photography or authority
  do not belong in devotional verdicts, meanings, stories or diet guidance.
- Avoid vague essay constructions such as “many families,” “some communities,” or
  “one documented tradition” when a sourced affirmative formulation or clearly
  labelled tradition name is available.
- Never describe devotional practice in a way that discourages worship. When an
  elaborate priest-led or temple rite cannot responsibly be reproduced at home,
  provide the complete simple household path first, then briefly label the larger
  rite as temple-led without scolding the reader.

### Safety placement

Safety is not a mandatory content section. Render it only when the specific
observance has a material, non-obvious risk—for example a strict fast, open fire,
deep-water immersion, food-storage risk or strenuous pilgrimage. Keep it short,
specific and separate from the devotional flow. Generic website-wide cautionary
boilerplate must not appear on every festival page.

Medical accuracy remains mandatory, but a medical note must not dominate or weaken
the religious guide. Common-sense event-management warnings such as “visit only
when safely accessible” are unnecessary.

## Religious rule first; safety note separately

1. State the traditional rule clearly. For example, where Hartalika Teej is
   traditionally observed as a nirjala fast, say so plainly.
2. If health context is needed, put it in a visibly separate **Health note**. The
   note must not redefine the vrat, call the traditional rule misleading, or
   present a modified practice as though it were the same religious rule.
3. A health note may advise a user to seek medical and family/religious guidance
   when the traditional observance is unsafe for that person. The choice remains
   with the user.
4. Never measure, rank, or question a user's devotion.

## Variants and disagreements

- Label regional, family, sampradaya, temple, and textual variants explicitly.
- If two established rules differ, show both and explain who follows each. Do not
  silently blend them or let Ganak choose a belief system for the user.
- Keep distinct observances distinct when their purpose, sequence, ending rites,
  or user expectations differ. Chaitra Navratri and Sharad Navratri therefore
  require separate user-facing guides.

## Changes that require owner approval

Agents must ask the owner before they:

- describe a core religious practice as optional, unnecessary, outdated, unsafe,
  or merely symbolic;
- replace a strict fast with fruit, water, or another modified observance;
- remove or soften a traditional prohibition or required ritual;
- merge two distinct festivals, seasons, traditions, or regional practices;
- choose one lineage or regional rule while hiding another established rule; or
- add a product opinion that changes how a user would understand or perform the
  observance.

Agents may correct spelling, broken links, missing card connections, and clearly
separate medical disclaimers without changing the religious instruction. Every
substantive religious-content change remains source-backed and is shown to the
owner during content review.

## Corpus-level completion gate

A festival/vrat content pass is not complete merely because every schema field is
non-empty. Before closure, review every public guide in English and Hindi for:

1. a distinct, uplifting answer-first verdict;
2. specific worship preparation and ordered steps;
3. suitable offerings, prayer/text/story and completion guidance;
4. affirmative diet/fast wording;
5. only useful, named tradition choices;
6. no internal research commentary or generic cautionary boilerplate;
7. optional safety rendered only for a specific material risk; and
8. no copied paragraph that could be moved to another festival unchanged.
